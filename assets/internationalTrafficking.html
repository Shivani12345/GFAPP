International trafficking victims

Traffickers – in the form of individuals or agencies – typically exploit Ghanaians looking to earn more money abroad.

Often the victims are women.

Recruitment agencies can deceive Ghanaian women promising work which is different to the reality of what they’ll do on arrival

These unlicensed agencies advertise on radio, television, social media, and newspapers. It can be difficult to work out if agencies are authentic.

Sometimes victims arrive in Ghana by plane and are then trafficked to other destinations. Ghanaian women are also transported to other parts of Africa (especially Nigeria, Côte D’Ivoire, Burkina Faso, the Gambia, and South Africa) as well as further afield.

Another typical scenario is that when promised jobs abroad don't materialise, female victims are forced into to survive or pay their debts to traffickers.

In addition to the GPS' anti trafficking unit, these crimes are policed by the Anti-Human Smuggling and Trafficking Unit (AHSTIP) of the Ghana Immigration Service.

Potential victims can be identified by

  • Sudden or unexpected plans to travel abroad
  • No previous connections abroad, or desire to go abroad

Case study

A Ghanaian woman is trafficked to Lebanon where she works for four abusive employers, and then struggles to get home.

  • Kisi from Ghana wanted work abroad to support her children.
  • She arrived in Lebanon in 2019 to work as a domestic worker, after getting help from an agent.
  • “He told me that Lebanon was a good country and that I wouldn’t have any problems. He told me that I could pay him once I reached Lebanon. He finished the visa process and bought me the air ticket.”
  • Kisi worked for several employers, who all exploited her.
  • Her first employer made her work 17-hour days, with no rest.
    • She was not allowed to contact her family.
    • She was not paid for three months.
  • She moved to a second employer in Lebanon.
    • Her second employer’s son beat her and threatened to kill her.
    • She was physically abused when she begged to leave.
  • The third employer required her to clean four homes.
  • Kisi’s fourth employer felt threatened after her husband commented on Kisi’s figure. Kisi was then allowed one meal per day, at most.
    • Kisi recalls: “She (her employer) told me that she was afraid I could lure her husband away from her.”
    • When the couple fought, Kisi was not allowed any food.
    • For almost six months, Kisi was not paid.
  • Between each employer, Kisi would return to the recruitment agency office. There she was severely underfed.
  • She said: “I learned from the other women there that they intentionally starve women so that they become so desperate that they agree to leave the office and work as soon as a job offer comes”.
  • Kisi was desperate to return to Ghana. The agency told her father he would have to pay $10,500 for her to leave. “I was told there was no way I would return home.”
  • Kisi received help from an NGO who contacted her employer.
  • She was then subjected to verbal and physical abuse.
  • Her agent threatened to jail her, and her employer claimed they could get her father in Ghana killed.
  • After lengthy negotiations, the employer paid Kisi four months’ worth of salary and sent her back to the agent, who locked her in a room for a week and confiscated her phone.
  • Since her employer had not paid her for more than three consecutive months, they had broken her two-year contract. This gave her the right to leave. She was allowed to travel back to Ghana once her family bought her ticket.
